Research Summary for 2014-2015 

(To-be-Update)


My research generally lies in the intersection between text mining and machine learning. I am especially interested in mining structured information from text data to uncover semantic structures around entities and applying structured analysis over them, by discovering principle and designing novel computational models. My work leads to over 10 publications in top venues in data mining, data management and information retrieval areas, including KDD, WWW, WSDM and SIGMOD. [Slides][Poster]

In particular, my Ph.D. research in the year of 2014 and 2015 aims at developing general and effective computational models to: 1) construct information network by extracting semantic structures such as typed entities (e.g., people, product, scientific concepts), synonymous entities and textual relations between entities from massive unstructured text corpora; and 2). perform information filtering (e.g., recommendation) in information networks via incorporating query-specific signal. 

1. Mining Structures for Information Network Construction

Terms: Entity Synonym Discovery; Phrase Mining; Entity Recognition and Typing; Relation Phrase Clustering.
Publications: [WWW'15], [SIGMOD'15], [SDM'14], [SIGMOD'14 Demo]
Submission: [KDD'15]

Research on structure-rich data has already achieved great success in gaining insights and knowledge by analyzing structured/semi-structured information, e.g., mining heterogeneous information network. Unfortunately, the majority of massive amount of data in the real world, from news to social media, business, scientific documents and web pages are unstructured or loosely structured. So far there is still no general, clear path to turn massive, unstructured data into structured knowledge like information networks. My research along this line focuses on mining from massive text corpora different semantic structures such as quality phrases, entities of various semantic types (people, product, concepts) and textual relations between entities, and discovering entity synonyms to resolve entity mentions.

In the past year, I developed different domain-agnostic phrase mining algorithms [SIGMOD'15,SDM'14] to extract high quality content units from massive text corpora. By incorporating part-of-speech tag patterns, we are able to generate entity mention candidates without any human supervision. I proposed a novel relation phrase-based framework for entity recognition and typing with distant supervision (knowledge bases) [KDD'15]. To resolve extracted entity mentions into their referring entities, I designed a synonym discovery method for structured entities in heterogeneous graphs [WWW'15]. As a case study, I demonstrated how these techniques can be leveraged together to build a powerful exploration system for massive news corpora [SIGMOD'14].

2. Query-specific Recommendation Models for Heterogeneous Information Networks

Terms: Citation Recommendation; Personalized Entity Recommendation; search intent learning; heterogeneous networks
Publications: [KDD'14], [WSDM'14a], [WSDM'14b]

We live in the booming age of heterogeneous information networks--an ubiquitous and large-scale data structure that can be extracted or constructed from different data sources. A critical problem is to help users explore massive yet semantic-rich heterogeneous networks, find desired information and discover interesting knowledge. To achieve this goal, we can either actively look for content in information networks which may interest users based on their past behaviors (i.e., recommendation), or we can answer queries composed by users to express their information needs (i.e., search). Following this paradigm, I study entity recommendation and citation prediction in heterogeneous information networks to facilitate users' information and knowledge discovery process.

In the past year, I worked on developing query-specific models for entity recommendation problems [WSDM'14b] and citation prediction problem [KDD'14], by incorporating the principle that one should diversify the predictive models for different queries based on their characteristics (e.g., behaviors, interests). I further proposed a search intent learning approach by leveraging Wikipedia concepts to understand user search intents behind their web queries [WSDM'14a].
